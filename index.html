The Price Question

What you're actually paying for.

Premium pricing should buy premium formulations — not premium marketing. Here's what the prices actually fund.

01 · The Formulas

Benchmark architectures, not bargain copies.

The House Defense uses the same C E Ferulic architecture — L-ascorbic acid + ferulic acid + vitamin E — that SkinCeuticals sells at $185. Ours is $74. The House Firm uses an advanced peptide blend where the cult-status DTC peptide serums at Sephora rely on three peptides at lower doses. Bounce stacks five actives where the most-Googled premium retinol — a single-active formula at $74 — uses just one. On the supplement side: clinical-grade ashwagandha (the form behind every cortisol study), magnesium glycinate (the chelated form that actually absorbs), multi-strain probiotics with enteric delivery for stomach-acid survival. Every formula is built to compound.

02 · The Ingredients

Clinical doses, no fairy-dust.

Most brands cut margins by under-dosing their hero actives. We don't. Skincare: niacinamide at clinically-validated concentrations, L-ascorbic acid at the CE Ferulic levels, retinol at concentrations matched to a buffering complex so tolerance is built in. Supplements: ashwagandha at the doses validated in cortisol-reduction trials, omega-3 at therapeutic EPA/DHA levels, NAD+ at a dose that actually moves cellular biomarkers, vitamin D3 paired with K2 for proper calcium routing. Trace amounts on the label aren't on the price tag here.

03 · The Manufacturing

The facility tier you'd expect from luxury brands.

Skincare is manufactured at FDA-registered, GMP-certified cosmetic CMs in Florida and Texas — the same caliber of contract manufacturer that supplies the prestige skincare and clean-beauty brands you'd find on the shelves of America's top specialty beauty retailers. Supplements are produced at an FDA-registered, cGMP-certified facility in Florida whose other clients sit on the shelves of the country's leading natural-grocery chains, with a documented NSF/ANSI 455-2 graduation path planned for hero SKUs in Year 2. Same facilities, same standards, same third-party batch testing — without the retail markup that shelf placement requires. The markup we're not charging is what you don't have to pay.

The Research

What the science says.

Every active in the Daniel's House line is built on peer-reviewed clinical evidence. Here's what the most current research shows.

Study 01
Ashwagandha

A 2025 systematic review and meta-analysis of 15 randomized controlled trials covering 873 patients found ashwagandha supplementation produces significant reductions in cortisol, stress, and anxiety versus placebo.

BJPsych Open, 2025 — Bachour et al.
Study 02
Creatine Monohydrate

A 2024 systematic review and meta-analysis of 16 randomized controlled trials confirmed creatine supplementation produces measurable benefits for memory, attention, and information processing speed — adding to decades of evidence for muscle and strength gains.

Frontiers in Nutrition, 2024 — Xu et al.
Study 03
Topical Retinoids

Topical retinoids have multi-decade clinical evidence for reducing fine lines, improving skin texture, and stimulating collagen production — among the most-studied actives in dermatology.

Reviewed in JAAD, 1986–present
Study 04
Magnesium Glycinate

Magnesium glycinate — the chelated, bioavailable form — is clinically associated with improved sleep quality, reduced stress markers, and better muscle recovery. Roughly 50% of US adults are deficient, and the glycinate form absorbs without the GI side effects of cheaper forms.

NIH ODS · Reviewed in Nutrients
Study 05
Niacinamide + Hyaluronic Acid

In a 2024 study of 44 women, daily niacinamide + hyaluronic acid serum significantly reduced wrinkle depth and improved skin elasticity within 8 weeks — the two ingredients create a measurable synergy on tone, texture, and barrier hydration.

Nature Scientific Reports, 2024
Study 06
Vitamin D3

Vitamin D3 has decades of clinical evidence for bone density, immune balance, and mood regulation. An estimated 35% of US adults are deficient — supplementation reliably closes the gap, particularly for those with limited sun exposure.

Reviewed in JAMA, The Lancet, NIH ODS
